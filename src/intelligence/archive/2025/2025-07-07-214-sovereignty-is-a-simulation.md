To join us, email metaviews at gmail.com or join our Signal chat, also done by sending an email.</strong></em></p><div><hr></div><p>Sovereignty is one of the most powerful fictions in modern politics. It implies control. Borders. Self-rule. The ability to say no.</p><p>But the world today is structured to render sovereignty obsolete. It’s the outcome of design: technological, economic, and strategic.</p><h2>China hacked Russia’s war machine</h2><p>In June, cybersecurity analysts confirmed that <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/19/world/europe/china-hackers-russia-war-ukraine.html">China has been systematically breaching Russia’s military and defense systems</a>. Despite public declarations of a “no-limits partnership,” Chinese intelligence services infiltrated Russian networks to extract information about battlefield strategy, drone use, and nuclear submarine capabilities.</p><p>Russia’s own FSB labeled China an “enemy” in a leaked internal memo. But nothing official was said. There were no expulsions. No retaliation. No press conference.</p><p>Russia couldn’t act—because it’s dependent. On Chinese credit, drones, and diplomatic cover. Its sovereignty is not real. It’s conditional.</p><p>At the same time, <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/23/canada-says-telcos-were-breached-in-china-linked-espionage-hacks/">Canada’s telecom networks were quietly compromised by long-dormant Chinese surveillance malware</a>. Cisco-made routers were manipulated to enable traffic collection and analysis, similar to hacks that targeted US telecoms.</p><p>The U.S. responded—not by offering help, but by issuing thinly veiled threats: decouple from China, or face consequences. Washington wants loyalty. But Washington also buys lithium batteries, critical minerals, and electronics—<em>from China</em>.</p><h2>The U.S. depends on China to fight China</h2><p>This is the paradox at the heart of the American war machine: it cannot function without the very nation it sees as its primary threat.</p><p>More than 70% of U.S. weapons systems rely on Chinese components. Rare earths, semiconductors, battery-grade graphite. Even the F‑35 fighter jet requires materials refined in China.</p><p>Efforts to decouple are under way, but progress is slow. The defense supply chain remains deeply entangled.</p><p>The question is simple: <em>Can you be sovereign if your military logistics depend on your rival?</em></p><p>All three of these situations—China hacking Russia, surveilling Canada, supplying the U.S.—share a common thread: <strong>sovereignty no longer functions as a meaningful concept in a networked, interdependent world</strong>.</p><p>It’s not just about espionage. It’s about <em>design</em>. The global infrastructure of power—supply chains, cloud platforms, telecom backbones—is built for control, not autonomy.</p><p>Sovereignty used to mean the capacity to act without external interference. Today, it’s a performance. A myth. A holdover from a pre-network age.</p><p>What replaces sovereignty? Not empire, exactly—but something stranger.</p><p>We live in a world governed by:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Dependencies</strong>: technical, economic, and institutional.</p></li><li><p><strong>Interference</strong>: from friends and rivals alike.</p></li><li><p><strong>Obfuscation</strong>: where the real power lies in who can <em>see</em>, <em>exploit</em>, or <em>deny</em> the truth of the system.</p></li></ul><p>China’s cyber-espionage doesn’t just expose secrets—it exposes the <strong>illusion of control</strong>. Canada, Russia, and the U.S. all claim to be sovereign. None are.</p><h2>The future of authority isn't sovereign</h2><p>The sooner we move beyond sovereignty as a central organizing fiction, the sooner we can confront reality—and build new forms of resilience.</p><p>That may look like:</p><ul><li><p>Transparent, federated systems of governance</p></li><li><p>Mutual aid networks across borders</p></li><li><p>Open source infrastructure immune to coercion</p></li><li><p>Collective security based on cooperation, not dependency</p></li></ul><p>Authority doesn’t disappear. But it evolves. It adapts. It decentralizes.</p><p>And if we’re serious about the future of authority, we must stop pretending sovereignty still exists in the form it once did.</p><p>It doesn't.</p><p>And the systems that still rely on it are collapsing from the inside.</p><div id="tiktok-iframe?media=1&amp;app=1&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.tiktok.com%2F%40mdg650hawk7thacct%2Fvideo%2F7523694237909536030&amp;key=e27c740634285c9ddc20db64f73358dd" class="tiktok-wrap outer" data-attrs="{&quot;url&quot;:&quot;https://www.tiktok.com/@mdg650hawk7thacct/video/7523694237909536030&quot;,&quot;title&quot;:&quot;This is when the really bad part starts &quot;,&quot;thumbnail_url&quot;:&quot;/assets/substack/214-sovereignty-is-a-simulation/3feb120b-7294-49af-be39-1a0c7ae999f8_1080x1920-4564b3c1.jpeg&quot;,&quot;author&quot;:&quot;mdg650hawk&quot;,&quot;embed_url&quot;:&quot;https://cdn.iframe.ly/api/iframe?media=1&amp;app=1&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.tiktok.com%2F%40mdg650hawk7thacct%2Fvideo%2F7523694237909536030&amp;key=e27c740634285c9ddc20db64f73358dd&quot;,&quot;author_url&quot;:&quot;https://www.tiktok.com/@mdg650hawk7thacct&quot;,&quot;belowTheFold&quot;:true}" data-component-name="TikTokCreateTikTokEmbed"><iframe id="iframe-tiktok-iframe?media=1&amp;app=1&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.tiktok.com%2F%40mdg650hawk7thacct%2Fvideo%2F7523694237909536030&amp;key=e27c740634285c9ddc20db64f73358dd" class="tiktok-iframe" src="https://cdn.iframe.ly/api/iframe?media=1&amp;app=1&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.tiktok.com%2F%40mdg650hawk7thacct%2Fvideo%2F7523694237909536030&amp;key=e27c740634285c9ddc20db64f73358dd" frameborder="0" allow="autoplay;
